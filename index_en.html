Competition Types

Ranking Tournament

A ranking tournament is an official UTTF tournament. Unless otherwise stated, you can register to a tournament by coming to the tournament 10‚Äì15 minutes before start.
A ranking tournament participation fee ranges from 150‚Ç¥ to 500‚Ç¥. Tournaments with small fees have symbolic prize money or none at all. Tournaments with big fees usually allocate half of the money the prize money.
To take part in a ranking tournament, players 18yo or older must pay the annual UTTF fee of 200‚Ç¥ (payment details).
If you don't have a UTTF ranking, and you win against a ranked player in a ranking tournament, UTTF will calculate and assign you a ranking.
Ranking tournaments have no age limits, except veterans tournaments (e.g., 40 y.o. and up, 50 y.o. and up, etc.) and kids tournaments (e.g., 2010 YOB, 2011 YOB, etc.).
For the reference:
  • You can find the official ranking of Ukrainian players, past game scores, tournaments, and participants on the Ukrainian Table Tennis Federation website.
  • You can calculate the UTTF ranking yourself in the ranking calculator.
Every tournament allows weak players to take part, even those who can't play at all.
However, every tournament has an upper limit to exclude players who are too strong. E.g.:
  • "Tournament for players without ranking" is a tournament for those who are just taking their first steps in table tennis or have played for a long time but cannot win against any ranked player.
  • "Tournament 0-8" is primarily for beginners, who have either no ranking (zero) or a very small official Ukrainian ranking (up to 8.9 inclusive);
  • "Tournament 0-15" is for beginners and amateurs;
  • "Tournament 0-40" is for beginners, amateurs, and professionals, though honestly, beginners would not be interested in such tournaments, since they will lose all their matches quickly and won't learn to play better.
  • "Tournament 0-100" is essentially a tournament without limits. Everyone can play, from novices to national team players.
Remarks
  • Tournaments in Volia, Orion, Raketka and Himars allow players to exceed the limit by a fraction. E.g., a player ranked 25.9 is allowed to take part in a "0-25" tournament. Pokolinnia doesn't allow any excess. Gourmet sometimes allows the excess, but not always.
  • Volia sometimes changes the limit of a tournament on its eve.
  • The attendance of tournaments in Volia is unpredictable: it often happens that just one or two players come to a tournament, and the tournament is canceled.

Non-Ranking Tournament

A non-ranking (aka amateur) tournament may or may not have a ranking limit. The ranking is used to seed players in both ranking and non-ranking tournaments. However:
  • Game results of non-ranking tournaments do not affect UTTF the rankings of players.
  • Participation in a non-ranking tournament does not require the annual UTTF fee.
  • Participation in a non-ranking tournament typically costs 30% less than participation in a ranking one.
  • Kids' non-ranking tournaments typically have age limits. Other non-ranking tournaments have no age limits.

Ladder Competition

A ladder competition is also known as a ladder tournament, a pyramid tournament, or "King of the Hill."

Advantages of this competition type are:

  • You can come alone; there is no need to have a partner.
  • You can join, leave, or take a break at any time. This does not interrupt the play for others.
  • You need not worry that your skill level might be low. After a few sets, strong players will play with strong players and weak players with weak ones.
  • The workout is very intensive since breaks between games are minimal.
  • Players are not as nervous as at a tournament. So, they overall swear less and behave better.
The competition goes like this:
  • Each table is assigned a number. The first table is the strongest, the second table is a bit weaker, and so on.
  • All tables begin to play a set at the same time
  • Players who finish their set wait for other players to finish theirs.
    • If many tables participate in the game, some clubs prefer to reduce the wait time by interrupting the game as soon as the strongest table (alternatively, any table) finishes its set. Players of that table must immediately announce, "Game over! Rotate!" All tables should stop immediately, even if some points are in progress. Players who lead their game are the winners. Tables, which had an equal score when their game was interrupted, play one final point (no-ad, "sudden death").
  • Then all players trade places. A player who lost the set goes to the table with the next highest number (that is, steps down to a lower rung of the ladder), thus playing the next set with a weaker opponent. A player who won the set goes to the next table with a smaller number (steps up to a higher rung of the ladder), thus playing the next set against a stronger player. As soon as everyone is ready to play the next set, the next set begins simultaneously on all tables.
  • Usually, such a competition lasts a few hours. However, all players play as long as they want. Those who join later or return after a break start to play from the lowest rung.
  • If the number of players is odd, the player who loses misses the next set (waits in line).
  • If there are only 3 players, then players alternate irrespective of who won or lost a set. Each player plays the first set with one opponent, the second set with another opponent, and then skips the third set. This order ensures equal distribution of playtime.
  • If there are not enough tables for all players (two or more players are in line), then two or more players, who lost their set at the weakest tables, go to the waiting line, and players who waited in line go to the tables.

Coach-Guided Matches

Coach-Guided Matches is a series of matches with pairings decided by a coach or organizer rather than automatic based on results.
In some clubs, this format resembles a ladder competition; in others, it‚Äôs more like a round-robin; and in some, it follows a tournament bracket structure.
From the perspective of stronger players, on one hand, the opponent rotation is not optimal, as they are sometimes paired with noticeably weaker players; but on the other hand, it gives them a chance to relax or experiment ‚Äî for example, by playing in a different style or with their non-dominant hand.
From the perspective of weaker players, it‚Äôs an appealing format because it offers the opportunity to play against much stronger players.

  • Table tennis or ping-pong?
    • Table tennis is an Olympic sport in which the ball travels fast and spins intensely, and players' rackets differ greatly in their characteristics‚Äîsuch as speed, control, tackiness, and grip on the ball. Each player selects a blade and rubber sheets that suit their playing style (attack, defence, or control). Often, the rubber on one side of the racket is very different from the rubber on the other, which is why, according to the rules, the rubbers must be of different colours.
    • The term "ping-pong" has a more complex history. Simply put, about a hundred years ago it was an alternative name for table tennis. Later, the term was used for recreational forms of table tennis. About 15 years ago, "ping-pong" became associated with a specific variation of table tennis in which players use identical rackets covered with sandpaper on both sides. In ping-pong, the ball usually travels more slowly and spins less than in table tennis.
