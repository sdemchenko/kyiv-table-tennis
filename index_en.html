Competition Types

Ranking Tournament

A ranking tournament is an official UTTF tournament. Unless otherwise stated, you can register to a tournament by coming to the tournament 10-15 minutes before start.
A ranking tournament participation fee ranges from 150‚Ç¥ to 500‚Ç¥. Tournaments with small fees have symbolic prize money or none at all. Tournaments with big fees usually allocate half of the money the prize money.
Besides, players 18yo or older must pay the annual UTTF fee of 200‚Ç¥. Normally this fee is paid at the beginning of the year (see payment details).
If you don't have a UTTF ranking, and you win against a ranked player in a ranking tournament, UTTF will calculate and assign you a ranking.
Ranking tournaments have no age limits, except veterans tournaments (e.g. 40 y.o. and up, 50 y.o. and up, etc.) and kids tournaments (e.g. 2010 YOB, 2011 YOB, etc.).
For the reference:
  • You can find the official ranking of Ukrainian players, past game scores, tournaments, and participants on the Ukrainian Table Tennis Federation website.
  • You can calculate the UTTF ranking yourself in the ranking calculator.
Every tournament allows weak players to take part, even those who can't play at all.
However, every tournament has an upper limit to exclude players who are too strong. E.g.:
  • "Tournament for players without ranking" is a tournament for those who are just taking their first steps in table tennis, or have played for a long time but cannot win against any ranked player.
  • "Tournament 0-8" is primarily for beginners, who have either no ranking (zero) or a very small official Ukrainian ranking (up to 8.9 inclusive);
  • "Tournament 0-15" is for beginners and amateurs;
  • "Tournament 0-40" is for beginners, amateurs, and professionals, though honestly, beginners would not be interested in such tournaments, since they will lose all their matches quickly and won't learn to play better.
  • "Tournament 0-100" is essentially a tournament without limits. Everyone can play, from novices to national team players.
Remarks
  • Tournaments in "Volia", "Orion", "Raketka" and "Himars" allow players to exceed the limit by a fraction. E.g., a player ranked 25.9 is allowed to take part in a "0-25" tournament. "Pokolinnia" doesn't allow any excess. "Gourmet" sometimes allows the excess, but not always.
  • "Volia" sometimes changes the limit of a tournament on its eve.
  • The attendance of tournaments in "Volia" is unpredictable: it often happens that just one or two players come to a tournament, and the tournament is canceled.

Non-Ranking Tournament

A non-ranking (aka amateur) tournament may or may not have a ranking limit. The ranking is used to seed players in both ranking and non-ranking tournaments. However:
  • Game results of non-ranking tournaments do not affect UTTF the rankings of players.
  • Participation in a non-ranking tournament does not require the annual UTTF fee.
  • Participation in a non-ranking tournament typically costs 30% less than participation in a ranking one.
  • Kids non-ranking tournaments typically have age limits. Other non-ranking tournaments have no age limits.

Ladder Competition

A ladder competition is also known as a ladder tournament, a pyramid tournament, "king of the hill", or a play for the strongest table.

The competition goes like this:

  • All tables are enumerated. The first table is the strongest, the second table is a bit weaker, and so on.
  • All tables begin to play a set at the same time
  • Players who finish their set wait for other players to finish theirs.
    • If many tables participate in the game, some clubs prefer to reduce the wait time by interrupting the game as soon as the strongest table (alternatively, any table) finishes its set. Players of that table must immediately announce "Finished! Change tables!". All tables should stop immediately, even if some points are in progress. Players who lead their game are the winners. Tables, which had an equal score when their game was interrupted, play one final point (no-ad, "sudden death").
  • Then all players trade places. A player who lost the set, goes to the table with the next higher number (that is, steps down to a lower rung of the ladder), thus playing the next set with a weaker opponent. A player who won the set, goes to the next table with a smaller number (steps up to a higher rung of the ladder), thus playing the next set against a stronger player. As soon as everyone is ready to play the next set, the next set begins simultaneously on all tables.
  • Usually, such competition lasts a few hours. However, all players play as long as they want. Those who join later or return after a break, start to play from the lowest rung.
  • If the number of players is odd, the player who loses misses the next set (waits in line).
  • If there are only 3 players, then players alternate irrespective of who won or lost a set. Each player plays the first set with one opponent, the second set with another opponent, and then skips the third set. This order ensures equal distribution of play time.
  • If there are not enough tables for all players (two or more players are in line), then two or more players, who lost their set at the weakest tables, go to the waiting line, and players who waited in line go to the tables.
Advantages of this kind of competition:
  • You can come alone; there is no need to have a partner.
  • You can join, leave, or take a break at any time. This does not interrupt the play for others.
  • You need not worry that your skill level might be low. After a few sets, strong players will play with strong players and weak players with weak ones.
  • The workout is very intensive since breaks between games are minimal.
  • Players are not as nervous as at a tournament. So, they overall swear less and behave better.

Group Games

It's a competition in a loose format. When done properly, it's a round-robin. In reality, it sometimes happens that strong players play with weak ones too much, or weak players constantly play with weak ones. That is, the rotation of opponents is not optimal.

    • Table tennis is an olympic sport. The ball flies fast and spins fast, while rackets of players have very different characteristics (speed, control, tackiness, grip, etc). All players choose the blade and rubbers matching their game style (attack, defence, control). Often the rubber on one side of the blade has very different characteristics from that on the other side, therefore the rules require that the rubbers must be of different color.
      • Newcomers who want a cheap quality racket can get a personally tested racket, which plays well both in clubs and outdoors. It is great for spins and smashes, it's not too fast and not too slow, albeit on the heavy side (188 grams). If you play outdoors, wipe the dust from the rubbers every 2-3 points.
        You can assemble even a better racket just as cheaply, but you'll have to buy the blade, the rubbers, and the glue, and then glue and cut the rubbers yourself.
    • The name "ping pong" has a more complex history. Simply put, a hundred years ago it was an alternative name for table tennis. After that, the term was primarily used for recreational variations of table tennis. About 15 years ago, the name "ping pong" was fixed to a table tennis variation, where players play with identical rackets having sandpaper on both sides. In ping pong, the ball flies slower and rotates less than in table tennis.
