Competition Types

Tournament

Unless otherwise stated, you can register to a tournament by coming to the tournament 10‚Äì15 minutes before start.
A tournament participation fee ranges from 150‚Ç¥ to 500‚Ç¥. Tournaments with small fees have symbolic prize money or none at all. Tournaments with big fees usually allocate half of the money the prize money.
Every tournament allows weak players to take part, even those who can't play at all.
However, every tournament has an upper limit to exclude players who are too strong. E.g.:
  • "Tournament for players without ranking" is a tournament for those who are just taking their first steps in table tennis or have played for a long time but cannot win against any ranked player.
  • "Tournament 0-8" is primarily for beginners, who have either no ranking (zero) or a very small official Ukrainian ranking (up to 8.9 inclusive);
  • "Tournament 0-15" is for beginners and amateurs;
  • "Tournament 0-40" is for beginners, amateurs, and professionals, though honestly, beginners would not be interested in such tournaments, since they will lose all their matches quickly and won't learn to play better.
  • "Tournament 0-100" is essentially a tournament without limits. Everyone can play, from novices to national team players.

Ranking Tournament

A ranking tournament is an official UTTF tournament.
To take part in a ranking tournament, players 18yo or older must pay the annual UTTF fee of 200‚Ç¥ (payment details).
If you don't have a UTTF ranking, and you win against a ranked player in a ranking tournament, UTTF will calculate and assign you a ranking.
Children's ranking tournaments are often held in several categories, e.g. children born in 2010 and older, children born in 2012 and older, etc (or U11, U13, U15, etc).
Likewise, ranking tournaments among table tennis veterans are often held in several categories: 40 years and older, 50 years and older, and so on.

For the reference:

  • You can find the official ranking of Ukrainian players, past game scores, tournaments, and participants on the Ukrainian Table Tennis Federation website.
  • You can calculate the UTTF ranking yourself in the ranking calculator.
  • Rules of table tennis sports competitions.
  • Unified sports classification of Ukraine for table tennis.
Remarks
  • Almost all clubs allow players whose rating exceeds the limit by tenths to participate in the tournament. E.g. a player ranked 25.9 is allowed to take part in a "0-25" tournament.
    Pokolinnia doesn't allow any excesses. Gourmet sometimes allows the excess, but not always.

Non-Ranking Tournament

The ranking is used to seed players in both ranking and non-ranking tournaments. However:
  • Game results of non-ranking tournaments do not affect UTTF the rankings of players.
  • Participation in a non-ranking tournament does not require the annual UTTF fee.
  • Participation in a non-ranking tournament typically costs 30% less than participation in a ranking one.
  • Non-ranking tournaments are divided into age categories much less often than ranking tournaments.

Ladder Competition

A ladder competition is also known as a ladder tournament, a pyramid tournament, or "King of the Hill."

Advantages of this competition type are:

  • You can come alone; there is no need to have a partner.
  • You can join, leave, or take a break at any time. This does not interrupt the play for others.
  • You need not worry that your skill level might be low. After a few sets, strong players will play with strong players and weak players with weak ones.
  • The workout is very intensive since breaks between games are minimal.
  • Players are not as nervous as at a tournament. So, they overall swear less and behave better.
Here‚Äôs how the competition works:
  • Each table is assigned a number, with Table 1 being the strongest, Table 2 slightly weaker, and so on in descending order of strength.
  • All tables start their sets simultaneously.
  • Players who complete their set shall wait until all other tables have finished.
    • When many tables are in use, some clubs shorten waiting times by ending all games as soon as the strongest table (or, alternatively, any table) finishes its set. The players at that table shall immediately announce, ‚ÄúGame over! Rotate!‚Äù Upon this call, play at all tables shall stop immediately, even if a rally is in progress. The leading player at each table is declared the winner. If the score is tied when play is stopped, one deciding point (no-ad, ‚Äúsudden death‚Äù) shall be played to determine the winner.
  • After each set, all players change tables. The player who lost the set moves to the table with the next higher number (stepping down to a lower rung of the ladder) and will face a weaker opponent in the next set. The player who won the set moves to the table with the next lower number (stepping up to a higher rung of the ladder) and will face a stronger opponent. Once all players are in position, the next set begins simultaneously on all tables.
  • Such competitions typically last several hours; however, players may continue for as long as they wish. Any player who joins late or returns after a break shall resume play from the lowest rung.
  • If the number of players is odd, the player who loses a set shall sit out the next set and join the waiting line.
  • If there are only three players, they rotate regardless of who won or lost the previous set. Each player faces one opponent in the first set, the other opponent in the second set, and then sits out the third set. This rotation ensures all players get equal playtime.
  • When there are insufficient tables for all players (i.e. two or more players are waiting), the players who lose their set on the lowest-ranked tables shall vacate their tables and join the waiting line. The players at the front of the waiting line shall then take their places at the vacated tables.

Coach-Guided Matches

Coach-Guided Matches is a series of matches with pairings decided by a coach or organizer rather than automatic based on results.
In some clubs, this format resembles a ladder competition; in others, it‚Äôs more like a round-robin; and in some, it follows a tournament bracket structure.
From the perspective of stronger players, on one hand, the opponent rotation is not optimal, as they are sometimes paired with noticeably weaker players; but on the other hand, it gives them a chance to relax or experiment ‚Äî for example, by playing in a different style or with their non-dominant hand.
From the perspective of weaker players, it‚Äôs an appealing format because it offers the opportunity to play against much stronger players.
